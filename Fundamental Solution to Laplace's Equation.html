In this article, we shall derive a fundamental solution to Laplace’s equation, which arises in many fields such as in differential geometry, electrostatics, and fluid mechanics.

1.1 Laplace’s Equation

Laplace’s equation is

\[ -\Delta u=0. \] which is a special case of the more general Poisson’s equation

\[ -\Delta u=f. \]

where \(f\) is called the source term.

1.2 Physical Interpretation

Let \(\vec F = -\text{grad}(u)\) be a smooth vector field defined on \(\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\). Suppose that the flux of \(\vec F\) across the boundary \(\partial \Omega\),

\[ \int_{\partial \Omega} \vec F \cdot \hat n \, dS, \]

is negligible. By the Divergence, theorem, the flux (or boundary) integral is equivalent to the bulk (or domain) integral, so that

\[ \int_{\partial\Omega}\vec F\cdot \hat n\,dS = \int_\Omega\text{div}(\vec F)\,d\vec x =0. \]

Since

\[ \begin{align} \text{div}(\vec F) &=\text{div}\big(-\text{grad}(u))\\[0.2cm] &=-\nabla\cdot\nabla u\\[0.2cm] &=-\nabla^2u\\[0.2cm] &=-\Delta u, \end{align} \] the integrand of the bulk term must satisfy

\[ -\Delta u=0. \]

In applications, \(u\) may represent some kind of density or potential. For example, if \(u\) represents

\[ \displaystyle \begin{cases} \text{chemical concentration}\\ \text{temperature}\\ \text{electrostatic potential}. \end{cases} \] then \(\vec F = -\text{grad}(u)\) is called

\[ \displaystyle \begin{cases} \text{Fick's law}\\ \text{Fourier's law of heat}\\ \text{Ohm's law}. \end{cases} \]

1.3 Strategy for Finding a Fundamental Solution

Laplace’s equation is a linear partial differential equation (PDE). To derive a fundamental solution, we exploit the symmetries of the Laplace operator.

The Laplacian is invariant under all Euclidean transformations, including:

  • rotations
  • translations
  • reflections

(All proofs left to the audience as an exercise).

Because of these symmetries, a fundamental solution centered at the origin must depend only on the distance from the origin, i.e., on \(|x|\), and not on the direction.

1.4 Derivation of Fundamental Solution

Let \(U=\mathbb R^n\) and \(x=(x_1,x_2,...,x_n)\). Since the Laplacian in invariant under rotations, we begin by looking for special solutions of the form

\[ u(x)=v(r), \]

called radial solutions, where \[ r=|x|=(x_1^2+x_2^2+\cdots+x_n^2)^{1/2} \]

is the standard Euclidean norm.

For each \(i=1,...,n\), we have

\[ \begin{align} \frac{\partial r}{\partial x_i} &=\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\bigg\{\;(x_1^2+x_2^2+\cdots+x_n^2)^{1/2}\;\bigg\}\\[0.2cm] &=\frac{1}{2}(x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_n)^{-1/2}\cdot(2x_i)\\[0.2cm] &=(x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_n)^{-1/2}\cdot x_i\\[0.2cm] &=\bigg((x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_n)^{1/2}\bigg)^{-1}\cdot x_i\\[0.2cm] &=(r)^{-1}\cdot x_i\\[0.2cm] &=\frac{x_i}{r}. \end{align} \] and

\[ \begin{align} \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\bigg\{\frac{x_i}{r}\bigg\} &=\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\bigg \{x_i \cdot \frac{1}{r}\bigg\}\\[0.2cm] &=\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\big\{x_i\big\}\cdot\frac{1}{r} \;+\; x_i\cdot\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\bigg\{\frac{1}{r}\bigg\}\\[0.2cm] &=(1)\cdot\frac{1}{r} \;+\; x_i\cdot\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\{r^{-1}\}\\[0.2cm] &=\frac{1}{r} \;+\; x_i\cdot (-1)\,\cdot r^{-2}\cdot\frac{x_i}{r}\\[0.2cm] &=\frac{1}{r} \;-\; \frac{x_i^2}{r^3}. \end{align} \]

Now, we assemble \(\Delta u\).

\(\bullet\) Getting \(u_{x_i}\)’s:

We have

\[ \begin{align} \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}u &=\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i} v =\frac{\partial v}{\partial r}\frac{\partial r}{\partial x_i}. \end{align} \] by chain rule.

Using subscript notation and letting \((\;'=\frac{\partial}{\partial r})\), we write

\[ \begin{align} u_{x_i} &=v'(r)\frac{\partial r}{\partial x_i} =v'(r)\frac{x_i}{r}. \end{align} \]

\(\bullet\) Getting \(u_{x_ix_i}\)’s:

Differentiate the previous line with respect to \(x_i\) once more:

\[ \begin{align} u_{x_ix_i} &=\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\bigg\{\,v'(r)\cdot\frac{x_i}{r}\,\bigg\}\\[0.2cm] &=\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\big\{\,v'(r)\,\big\}\cdot\frac{x_i}{r}\;+\;v'(r)\cdot\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\bigg\{\frac{x_i}{r}\bigg\}\\[0.2cm] &=v''(r)\,\frac{x_i^2}{r^2} \;+\; v'(r)\,\bigg(\frac{1}{r}-\frac{x_i^2}{r^3}\bigg). \end{align} \]

\(\bullet\) Getting \(\Delta u\):

To get \(\Delta u\) we sum all \(u_{x_ix_i}\)’s for \(i=1,...,n\):

\[ \begin{align} \Delta u &=\sum_{i=1}^n u_{x_ix_i}\\[0.2cm] &=\sum_{i=1}^n\bigg\{\,v''(r)\frac{x_i^2}{r^2} \;+\;v'(r)\bigg(\frac{1}{r}-\frac{x_i^2}{r^3}\bigg)\,\bigg\}\\[0.2cm] &=\sum_{i=1}^n v''(r)\frac{x_i^2}{r^2} \;+\;\sum_{i=1}^n v'(r)\frac{1}{r} \;-\;\sum_{i=1}^n v'(r)\frac{x_i^2}{r^3}\\[0.2cm] &=\frac{v''(r)}{r^2}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 \;+\;\frac{nv'(r)}{r} \;-\;\frac{v'(r)}{r^3}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2\\[0.2cm] &=\frac{v''(r)}{r^2}\cdot r^2 \;+\;\frac{nv'(r)}{r} \;-\;\frac{v'(r)}{r^3}\cdot r^2\\[0.2cm] &=v''(r)\;+\;\frac{nv'(r)}{r}\;-\;\frac{v'(r)}{r}\\[0.2cm] \Delta u&=v''(r)+\frac{n-1}{r}v'(r). \end{align} \]

Thus, \(\Delta u = 0\) holds if and only if

\[ v''(r)+\frac{n-1}{r}\,v'(r)=0 \quad\text{for}\quad r>0, \]

which is a linear second order ordinary differential equation (ODE). We now focus on solving this ODE.

1.5 Solving the ODE in \(r\)

For the purposes of analysis, we first reduce the second order ODE to an equivalent first-order equation as follows:

\[ \begin{align} v''+\frac{n-1}{r}v'&=0\\[0.2cm] \frac{v''}{v'}&=-\frac{n-1}{r}\\[0.2cm] \frac{v''}{v'}&=\frac{1-n}{r}\\[0.2cm] \implies\quad \log(|v'|)'&=\frac{1-n}{r}. \end{align} \]

Integrating the last line once with respect to \(r\) yields

\[ \begin{align} \log(|v'|) &=(1-n)\log r+\text{constant}\\[0.2cm]\tag{by properties of logorithms} &=\log r^{(1-n)}+\text{constant} \end{align} \]

Taking the exponential of this new expression further yields

\[ \begin{align} |v'|&=\exp\big\{\;\log r^{(1-n)}+\text{constant}\;\big\}\\[0.2cm]\tag{by properties of exponents} &=\exp\big\{\log r^{(1-n)}\big\}\cdot\exp\{\;\text{constant}\;\}\\[0.2cm] \implies\quad v'&=Kr^{(1-n)}\\[0.2cm] &=Kr^{-(n-1)}\\[0.2cm] \implies\quad v'(r)&=\frac{K}{r^{n-1}}, \end{align} \] for \(K\) a constant.

We now proceed with the case analysis:

\(\bullet\) Case \(n=1\)

We have

\[ v'(r)=K. \] The solution consists of a family of straight lines. Namely, \[ v(r)=Kr+L, \] for constants \(K\) and \(L\).

\(\bullet\) Case \(n=2\)

In this case, the equation becomes

\[ v'(r)=\frac{K}{r} \] which has the solution

\[ v(r)=K\log r + L \quad\text{for}\quad r>0 \] and constants \(K\) and \(L\).

\(\bullet\) Case \(n\ge3\)

Notice that for

\[ \begin{aligned} n=3:\quad\quad v'=\frac{K}{r^2} &\quad\implies\quad v=-\frac{K}{r}+\text{constant} \\[0.2cm] n=4:\quad\quad v'=\frac{K}{r^3} &\quad\implies\quad v=-\frac{1}{2}\frac{K}{r^2}+\text{constant} \\[0.2cm] n=5:\quad\quad v'=\frac{K}{r^4} &\quad\implies\quad v=-\frac{1}{3}\frac{K}{r^3}+\text{constant} \\[0.2cm] n=6:\quad\quad v'=\frac{K}{r^5} &\quad\implies\quad v=-\frac{1}{4}\frac{K}{r^4}+\text{constant}\\[0.2cm] n=7:\quad\quad v'=\frac{K}{r^6} &\quad\implies\quad v=-\frac{1}{5}\frac{K}{r^5}+\text{constant}\\[0.2cm] n=8:\quad\quad v'=\frac{K}{r^7} &\quad\implies\quad v=-\frac{1}{6}\frac{K}{r^6}+\text{constant}, \end{aligned} \] and so on.

Thus, for \(n\ge 3\), the solutions take the form

\[ v(r)=-\frac{1}{n-2}\,\frac{K}{r^{n-2}} \;+\; L \quad\text{for}\quad r>0 \]

and constants \(K\) and \(L\).

1.6 Assembling Solutions

Putting all solutions together give us

\[ \begin{align} \displaystyle v(r)= \begin{cases} Kr \;+\; L&\text{if}\quad\quad n=1\\[0.2cm] K\log r \;+\; L&\text{if}\quad\quad n=2\\[0.2cm] -\frac{1}{n-2}\,\frac{K}{r^{n-2}} \;+\; L&\text{if}\quad\quad n\ge3 \end{cases} \end{align} \]

for constants \(K\) and \(L\) and \(x\in\mathbb R^n\) with \(x\neq0\) whenever \(n\ge 2\).

Since \(r=|x|\), we may now represent the solution for the original dependent variable \(u\) as

\[ \begin{align}\displaystyle u(x)= \begin{cases} K|x| \;+\; L&\text{if}\quad\quad n=1\\[0.2cm] K\log|x| \;+\; L&\text{if}\quad\quad n=2\\[0.2cm] -\frac{1}{n-2}\,\frac{K}{|x|^{n-2}} \;+\; L&\text{if}\quad\quad n\ge3. \end{cases} \end{align} \]

for \(x\in\mathbb R^n\) with \(x\neq0\) whenever \(n\ge 2\).

1.7 The Fundamental Solution

It remains to find the constants \(K\) and \(L\).

Claim: There exists constants \(K\) and \(L\) such that the function \(u(x)\) satisfies

\[ -\Delta_x u = \delta_0, \] where \(\delta_0\) denotes the Dirac delta at the origin.

Mathematically, it is a natural choice to define the Laplacian of \(u\) at the origin as a Dirac delta because it captures the singular behavior of the solution when \(n \ge 2\).

Physically, the Dirac delta models the impulse response of a system, i.e., the effect of a unit point source located at the origin, such as a point mass or a point charge.

\(\bullet\) Finding \(L\)

The constant \(L\) has no effect on the solution for all \(n\). To see this, suppose we add a constant \(L\) to the singular part of the solution:

\[ u(x) = u_{\text{singular}}(x) + L. \]

Then

\[ \begin{align} -\Delta u &= -\Delta (u_{\text{singular}} + L)\\[0.2cm] &= -\Delta u_{\text{singular}} - \Delta L\\[0.2cm] &= -\Delta u_{\text{singular}} - 0\\[0.2cm] &= -\Delta u_{\text{singular}}\\[0.2cm] \implies\quad -\Delta u &= \delta_0 \end{align} \]

Thus, adding \(L\) does not change the Laplacian of the function \(u\) and therefore does not affect the condition \(-\Delta u = \delta_0\), so we set \(L=0\) to simplify the formula.

\(\bullet\) Finding \(K\)

We shall focus on finding \(K\) for the case when \(n=2\). We make use of the condition

\[ -\Delta u = \delta_0. \]

by integrating both sides over a small ball \(B_\varepsilon\) centered at the origin:

\[ \int_{B_\varepsilon} -\Delta u \, dx = \int_{B_\varepsilon} \delta_0 \, dx. \]

By the defining property of the Dirac delta,

\[ \int_{B_\varepsilon} \delta_0 \, dx = 1, \]

so

\[ \int_{B_\varepsilon} -\Delta u \, dx = 1. \]

By the divergence theorem \[ \int_{B_\varepsilon} -\Delta u \, dx = - \int_{\partial B_\varepsilon} \frac{\partial u}{\partial n} \, dS. \]

Thus we obtain the normalization condition \[ -\int_{\partial B_\varepsilon} \frac{\partial u}{\partial n} \, dS = 1. \]

For \(n=2\), we take \[ u(x)=K\log|x|. \]

Since the solution is radial, \[ \frac{\partial u}{\partial n} = \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\big\{K\log r\big\}=\frac{K}{r}. \]

On \(\partial B_\varepsilon\) we have \(r=\varepsilon\), so

\[ \frac{\partial u}{\partial n}=\frac{K}{\varepsilon}. \]

Since the circumference of \(\partial B_\varepsilon\) is \(2\pi\varepsilon\), the boundary integral becomes

\[ -\int_{\partial B_\varepsilon} \frac{\partial u}{\partial n} \, dS =-\frac{K}{\varepsilon}\int_{\partial B_\varepsilon}dS = -\frac{K}{\varepsilon}\cdot(2\pi\varepsilon) = -2\pi K. \]

Therefore \[ -2\pi K = 1, \]

or

\[ K = -\frac{1}{2\pi}. \]

We can now define

\[ \Phi(x) = \begin{cases} -\dfrac{1}{2}|x| & \text{if} \quad\quad n = 1,\\[0.2cm] -\dfrac{1}{2\pi} \log|x| & \text{if} \quad\quad n = 2,\\[0.2cm] \dfrac{1}{n(n-2)\,\alpha(n)} \dfrac{1}{|x|^{\,n-2}} & \text{if} \quad\quad n \ge 3, \end{cases} \quad\quad x \in \mathbb{R}^n - \{0\}, \] where \(\alpha(n)\) is the volume of the unit ball in \(\mathbb{R}^n\).

\(\Phi\) is called a fundamental solution of the Laplacian.
